Not Very Complex Numbers

A complex number is just a point on a plane. Two names for the same point: x + i y — go right x, up y — or r ↺ θ° — length r, then turn θ° anti-clockwise from directly right (the +1 direction) toward straight up (the +i direction). Drag the tips.

Teachers' lounge — take it to paper

Ruler & protractor (no formulas needed). Print the plane. Pick a point and measure its length r with a ruler and its angle θ with a protractor — anti-clockwise from the +1 line. Do your measurements match the readout? Where does measuring start to get hard?

Pythagoras. The two dashed legs are exactly x and y, and the arrow is the hypotenuse. So how could you get r from x and y with no ruler at all? Test your idea on 3 + 4i, then on 1 + i. (Hint: it's the theorem with the squares.)

Trigonometry. A point carries four numbers — x, y, r, θ — but only two are ever free; the other two follow. Which ratio turns an angle into x and y? Going the other way, which ratio gives θ from x and y, and which “inverse” key on the calculator finishes the job? (Hints only — let them find the formulas.)
